# The absurd process of marrying an Indian citizen in Germany

**TL;DR:** In the city of Bonn, it takes about one year and 700 Euro to finalize the marriage between a German and an Indian citizen.

My wife has lived in Germany for about a decade and we wanted to get married here. She is still an Indian citizen and this is a description of the process that we went through. Your mileage may vary. The fastest possible timeline that you could achieve will still take about 7 to 9 months.

## Timeline

- March 18, 2025. Email to the city of Bonn asking for an appointment to understand the process of getting married.
- March 28, 2025. Follow-up email since we got no answer to our first email.
- April 2, 2025. We are given an appointment for mid-April at the marriage registration office.
- April 17, 2025. Confusing conversation at their office.
- May 19, 2025. We got busy and life got in the way and we are now resuming our efforts. We write to the marriage registrar to clarify many of our questions around the concept of the "Ehefähigkeitszeugnis." We are informed about all the documents that we need to provide.
- June 2025. We are on vacation.
- July 28, 2025. We now have a clearer picture of the required documents. My wife's parents have to go to a notary to get some documents. We are also busy getting relevant documents.
- September 9, 2025. The documents have arrived from India.
- October 13, 2025. The clerk at the city hall was on vacation, but we now have an appointment. We are officially starting the process. I am actually not sure anymore which process this is. But my wife's documents were being sent to India so that someone can check on my wife's parents.
- December 10, 2025. A person comes to my wife's father's place. They talk and go to the notary with which the affidavit is signed. That person asks us to again provide him with a bunch of documents (CV of both of us. When, how we met, ...).
- February 6, 2026. The documents made their way back to Germany. They are now being sent to the Oberlandsgericht in Köln (higher regional court).

## Required documents

Obviously this is not legal advice. I might have forgotten documents. Your city might handle things differently. But here's what we had to provide.

- Me, a German citizen.
    - ID (Personalausweis)
    - Birth certificate (Abstammungsurkunde). I requested this from the city where I was born because I was not sure if this is identical to the Geburtsurkunde. I got sent the Abstammungsurkunde within a week.
    - Registration certificate (Erweiterte Meldebescheinigung). I requested this from the city I currently live in. This took about a week.
    - Salary slips. I printed the last half year of them.
    - 500 Euro. (Not in cash, but they will charge you.)
- My wife, an Indian citizen.
    - Passport
    - Visa (EU Blue Card in her case)
    - Affidavit from her parents, stating that she is not married.
    - Affidavit from her parents, stating that she is not married. Translated into German.
    - Birth certificate.
    - Birth certificate. Translated into German.
    - About half a dozen school certificates and university degrees. Anything that has her name on it and shows that she is a real person. The fact that Indian documents often abbreviate names did not help her. For example, for Jane Maria Doe, it would often just state Jane MD.
    - Salary slips. I printed the last half year of them.
    - University certificate. This was from a German university so it was already in German.
    - Government ID. This is India's Aadhaar card.
    - Tax card. From India's Income Tax Department.
    - Two biometric passport photos.
    - **NOT** a document from the Sarpanch. Initially, the clerk wanted to have a document from the Sarpanch ("Dorfälteste"), stating that my wife is not already married. The concept of the Sarpanch makes no sense if you are coming from a city of 10+ million citizens. After explaining this to the clerk, he suggested to get a document signed by the mayor. I pushed back (imagine having to get a document from the mayor of NYC, a smaller city, stating that you are not married). He did his inquiry and wrote to me a couple of days later that this was not necessary anymore.

## Remarks

- Don't shoot the messenger.
    - This was a very frustrating process. However, we got insanely lucky with the clerk that was assigned to our case. He was very rigorous and patiently explained to us the whole process. It was very annoying to be asked for all the documents, but he was just doing his job. And he did it thoroughly and properly.
- This was the meeting where we officially handed over all our documents and started the process of having our documents vetted by the German consulate in India, and then by the German Oberlandesgericht in Köln. During this meeting we had to sign an affidavit, stating basically that we are not lying about anything. This affidavit was in _legal German_. The clerk insisted that he needs to have the impression that my wife fully understands the affidavit. I told him that she speaks German at about B1/B2 level. He told me that this legal document might require you to have C1 level of German skills to properly understand it. I told him that I could translate. He said that I would not be allowed to translate (I guess I could lie and state that she gets a free car for marrying me). So we had to bring a friend of ours who sat through the whole process (2 hours). It turned out that my wife was able to understand the document so this friend also wasted two hours of his life. (Thanks Leo!)
- The person that was confirming the details of my wife back in India asked us to send documents to him. This all happened via WhatsApp and didn't feel great at all.
